The Lighthouse at Cape Verity

For nearly two hundred years, the lighthouse at Cape Verity has stood at the edge of the rocky coastline, its white tower rising one hundred and twelve feet above the crashing surf. It was built in 1827 by a crew of stonemasons who hauled granite blocks up the cliffside by rope and pulley, a process that took almost four years to complete. Local legend says that one of the masons, a man named Elias Burke, carved his initials into the keystone above the doorway. Visitors today can still find the faint letters "E.B." if they know where to look.

The original light was fueled by whale oil, which had to be carried up the spiral staircase in heavy brass canisters every evening. The keeper, who lived in a small cottage at the base of the tower, would climb the two hundred and fourteen steps twice a night to refill the lamp and trim the wick. In 1889, the lighthouse was converted to kerosene, and in 1932, it was finally electrified. The original Fresnel lens, hand-ground in Paris and shipped across the Atlantic in wooden crates, is still in operation today.

Storms along this stretch of coast can be ferocious. In the winter of 1898, a nor'easter battered the lighthouse for three straight days, and the keeper at the time, a woman named Margaret Hollis, kept the light burning despite losing power to the cottage and running dangerously low on supplies. Her journal, preserved in the local maritime museum, describes huddling near the stove in her oilskin coat, eating cold beans from a tin, and climbing the tower every two hours to make sure the lamp was still lit. Thirty-one ships were said to have passed safely that week because of her vigilance.

The lighthouse was automated in 1971, and the last full-time keeper, a soft-spoken man named Roy Pearson, retired the same year. He had served at Cape Verity for twenty-six years, and on his final night he reportedly climbed the tower one last time, sat on the gallery deck, and watched the beam sweep across the dark water until sunrise. A small bronze plaque near the entrance commemorates his service.

Today, the lighthouse is maintained by a nonprofit foundation and is open to the public on weekends from May through October. Tours are led by volunteer docents, many of whom are descendants of former keepers. Visitors can climb the original staircase, examine the Fresnel lens up close, and step out onto the gallery deck for a panoramic view of the coastline. On clear days, you can see all the way to Hadley Point, twelve miles to the north, where a smaller, decommissioned light station sits abandoned on a windswept bluff.

Marine biologists from the nearby university have also begun using the lighthouse grounds as a research station. They monitor seabird populations, track the movements of harbor seals along the rocks below, and study the effects of changing ocean temperatures on local kelp forests. Their work has revealed surprising patterns: puffin colonies that had not been seen in the region for decades have begun to return, while certain species of cold-water fish are migrating farther north each summer.

Whether you come for the history, the views, or the science, the lighthouse at Cape Verity has a way of making visitors slow down. There is something about standing at the top of that old stone tower, with the wind in your face and the sea stretching out to the horizon, that puts the small worries of daily life into a longer, quieter perspective.
